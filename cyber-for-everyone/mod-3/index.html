My Passwords

How do I Choose a Secure Password?

For many users, passwords are more of a nuisance than a security mechanism, and therefore, they use passwords that can be easily remembered i.e. easily guessed, such as:

  • The word "password"
  • The same as the username
  • Birthdays or places of birth
  • Family members
  • Pets
  • Colors, foods, favorite places, etc.

Common errors creating passwords

Error 1: Use Personal Information

Many people use personal info to create easy to remember passwords, for example, birth dates, ID number, name of husband, wife, children, relatives, pets, place of birth, and all the potential combinations; and because of this practice criminals steal our data!!!; it is relatively easy for the criminal to find this data on your Facebook page! or social networks, i.e.:

  • Amelia0910
  • Robert0414

Error 2: Passwords so Easy to guess

Create passwords that are too simple not to be forgotten; using words easily to find in a Dictionary; some of the most common passwords used by people are:

  • 123456
  • Password
  • 12345678
  • Qwerty
  • 12345
  • Abc123
  • Passw0rd

They also combine City, Country and year or month and year:

  • USA2023
  • Alabama2023
  • VirginiaUSA2023

Error 3: Reuse of Passwords

They use the same password for many services, so if the criminal manages to find it, much of your information will be compromised or a t risk.

Error 4: Reusing passwords with slight differences

In other words, they have 2 or three passwords and they change only one letter or symbol and with that they think they are secure or that the password is different; For example:

  • Virginia2023
  • Then… Virginia2023*
  • Then… Virginia2023-
  • Then… Virginia2023.

Error 5: Not changing passwords periodically

Changing passwords periodically can help prevent the use of stolen or compromised passwords obtained through data breaches or other means. If a user's password is stolen or compromised, changing it can help prevent unauthorized access to their accounts.

Overall, changing passwords periodically is a simple but effective security measure that can help protect against unauthorized access to personal or sensitive information. It is generally recommended to change passwords at least every six months, and to use strong, unique passwords for each account.

How does a Cybercriminal Crack My Password?

Passwords are generally cracked using one or more of the following methods:

Guessing: The offender based on the knowledge and research done on the person, tests words or values related to the victim manually.

Dictionary Attack: The offender makes a list of possible passwords (dictionary) and tries them one by one.

Brute Force: The offender uses tools that try all possible combinations of letters, numbers and symbols until the password is found.

I have a lot of passwords, what do I do?

There are specialized and relatively secure tools available called Password Managers. These are tools that allow us to store multiple passwords and manage them securely:

For Example:

  • Lastpass
  • Keepass

Recommendations:

  • Your LastPass master password must be sufficiently robust.
  • When you are finished using your computer, log out of LastPass, so that no one else can access your passwords; if you leave the session open, anyone with access to your computer could access your services.

Multifactor Authentication

A final recommendation about passwords and authentication in different portals or services is that you activate what is technically called "Multi Factor Authentication"; This means that to enter a service, not only the key is needed, but something additional, for example something you have (A Verification Number sent by message to your cell phone) or something you are (Biometric identification, for example, your fingerprint or the pupil of your eye, like in the movies or your face like in some cell phones); it's much more secure than just having a password.

Multifactor authentication is a security mechanism that uses several types of credentials to validate that a user is legitimate, usually combined using these factors.:

  • A physical object: (what we have) Card , token , USB , credential , among others
  • A knowledge factor: (what we know) credential, password, pin, among others
  • A biometric identifier: (what we are) fingerprint, iris, voice command, handwriting, among others

If one of the above factors does not match, access is blocked.

It is recommended to enable this mechanism to protect all our accounts.
