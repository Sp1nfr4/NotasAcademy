Deactivate the automatic content preview

It may sound a bit complicated, but this is very simple: have you noticed that many emails come to you with images, colors, forms, etc.; well, these contents often serve either to collect data about you, or in the worst case to infect your machine; so, it is best to only view these contents when they come from companies or people you trust.

The process to deactivate automated content preview in email clients may vary depending on the email client you are using. However, here are general steps that can guide you:

  1. Open your email client and go to the settings menu.
  1. Look for the "preview pane" or "reading pane" settings.
  1. Find the option to disable or turn off the content preview feature.
  1. Save your changes and exit the settings menu.

Note that the exact wording and location of these settings may differ depending on the email client you are using. If you're unsure of how to turn off content preview in your email client, you can refer to the help documentation or support website for your specific email client.

PGP - Pretty Good Privacy

PGP stands for "Pretty Good Privacy," and it is a computer program used for secure communication. PGP provides encryption and decryption of electronic messages, including email, text messages, and files, to protect the confidentiality and integrity of the information being transmitted. It uses a combination of symmetric and asymmetric encryption methods to protect the message contents and ensure that only the intended recipient can decrypt and read the message. PGP is widely used for secure communication in sensitive fields such as business, politics, and journalism.

Email Attacks

Phishing

Phishing attacks are a type of social engineering attack that involve sending fraudulent communications; the most used channel are Emails.

A Phishing email is designed to trick recipients into providing sensitive information, such as login credentials, credit card details, or other personal information. Phishing emails often appear to be from a trusted source, such as a bank or an employer.

Spear Phishing

Spear phishing is a type of cyber attack where a malicious actor sends a personalized, highly targeted email or message to an individual in order to trick them into divulging sensitive information or performing an action that is harmful to the individual or their organization.

Unlike regular phishing attacks that are mass-mailed to many people, spear phishing is highly customized to the individual and often uses information gleaned from social media or other online sources to make the message appear legitimate. The attacker may pose as a trusted authority figure or a colleague in order to gain the target's trust.

Spear phishing can take many forms, including email, instant messages, or even phone calls. It is often used as a way to gain access to corporate networks or steal sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, and financial data.

To protect against spear phishing, it's important to be wary of unsolicited messages, verify the identity of the sender, and avoid clicking on links or downloading attachments unless you're absolutely sure they're safe. Additionally, using strong passwords and two-factor authentication can help prevent unauthorized access to your accounts.

Whaling

Whaling is a specific type of phishing attack that targets high-level executives or individuals with access to sensitive information or financial resources within an organization.

Whaling attacks are similar to other types of phishing attacks in that they involve the use of fraudulent emails or messages designed to trick the recipient into divulging sensitive information, such as login credentials or financial data. However, what distinguishes whaling attacks from other phishing attacks is their focus on high-value targets, such as CEOs, CFOs, and other high-ranking executives.

Whaling attacks often involve a great deal of research and planning by the attacker, who may use social engineering techniques to create a sense of urgency or authority in their message. For example, the attacker may pose as a trusted authority figure or use spoofed email addresses to make the message appear more legitimate.

To protect against whaling attacks, it's important to be vigilant and skeptical of unsolicited messages, especially if they request sensitive information or require urgent action. Verifying the identity of the sender and using two-factor authentication can also help prevent unauthorized access to sensitive information or accounts. Additionally, organizations can implement security protocols and provide training to employees to help prevent and detect whaling attacks.

BEC Attack

BEC stands for Business Email Compromise, which is a type of cyber attack that involves the use of social engineering and deception to gain unauthorized access to business email accounts. BEC attacks are also known as CEO fraud or whaling attacks, as they often target high-level executives or individuals with access to financial resources within an organization.

In a BEC attack, the attacker typically poses as a trusted authority figure, such as a CEO, CFO, or vendor, and sends a fraudulent email or message to an employee within the organization. The message may ask the employee to perform a certain task, such as making a payment or sharing sensitive information, or may contain a link or attachment that installs malware on the victim's computer.

BEC attacks can be highly sophisticated and often involve a great deal of research and planning by the attacker. They may use social engineering techniques to create a sense of urgency or authority in their message, such as posing as a vendor who needs payment immediately or a CEO who needs to keep information confidential.

To protect against BEC attacks, it's important to be vigilant and skeptical of unsolicited messages, especially if they request sensitive information or require urgent action. Verifying the identity of the sender and using two-factor authentication can also help prevent unauthorized access to sensitive information or accounts. Additionally, organizations can implement security protocols and provide training to employees to help prevent and detect BEC attacks.

Spam

Spam attacks are a type of unsolicited mass email or message that are sent to a large number of people in order to advertise a product or service, distribute malware, or obtain sensitive information. Spam attacks can be delivered through email, instant messaging, social media, or other online communication channels.

Spam attacks are often annoying and disruptive to recipients, and can also pose a security threat if they contain malicious links or attachments. Spam filters are commonly used by email providers and other online platforms to automatically detect and block spam messages, but some spam messages may still make it through.

Spammers often obtain email addresses and other contact information through various means, including scraping web pages, purchasing lists, or using malware to harvest contact information from infected devices.

To protect against spam attacks, it's important to be cautious of unsolicited messages and avoid clicking on links or downloading attachments from unknown or suspicious sources. Using strong spam filters and anti-malware software can also help prevent spam messages from reaching your inbox and protect your device from malicious software. Additionally, being careful about sharing personal information online can help prevent spammers from obtaining your contact information in the first place.

Email Bombing

Email bombing is a type of cyber attack where an individual or group sends a large number of emails or messages to a specific email address or domain in order to overwhelm the recipient's inbox or server.

The purpose of email bombing may vary depending on the attacker's goals. In some cases, email bombing may be used as a form of harassment or to disrupt the normal functioning of a website or email server. In other cases, email bombing may be used as a form of extortion or to demand a ransom in exchange for stopping the attack.

Email bombing can be carried out using automated tools or scripts that send large volumes of emails at once. Attackers may also use botnets, which are networks of infected computers that can be controlled remotely to carry out coordinated attacks.

To protect against email bombing attacks, it's important to use strong spam filters and anti-malware software that can detect and block suspicious activity. It's also a good idea to use email services that provide protection against email bombing attacks and to avoid sharing your email address with unknown or untrusted sources. If you are the victim of an email bombing attack, it's important to report the incident to your email provider or IT department as soon as possible.

Ok, I feel super technical, what else can I do?

A mail header is like an envelope for your email. It contains information about who the email is from, who it is going to, the subject of the email, and other technical details that help the email get delivered correctly. Think of it like a label on a package that tells the postal service where to send it and who it's from. The mail header is usually hidden from view but can be accessed by looking at the email's properties or settings.

To access the mail header in an email, you can follow the steps below depending on the email client or service you are using:

Gmail:

  • Open the email message.
  • Click on the three vertical dots in the upper-right corner.
  • Select "Show original" from the dropdown menu.
  • The full message header will appear in a new window.

Outlook:

  • Open the email message.
  • Click on the three horizontal dots in the upper-right corner.
  • Select "View message details" from the dropdown menu.
  • The full message header will appear in a new window.

Apple Mail:

  • Open the email message.
  • Click on "View" in the menu bar at the top of the screen.
  • Select "Message" from the dropdown menu.
  • Select "All Headers" to view the full message header.

Yahoo Mail:

  • Open the email message.
  • Click on the three horizontal dots in the upper-right corner.
  • Select "View raw message" from the dropdown menu.
  • The full message header will appear in a new window.

Once you have accessed the message header, you can view information such as the sender, recipient, subject, date and time sent, and the path the email took to reach its destination.
